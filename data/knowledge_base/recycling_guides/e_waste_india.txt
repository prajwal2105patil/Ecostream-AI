E-WASTE (ELECTRONIC WASTE) DISPOSAL GUIDE FOR INDIA
Under E-Waste (Management) Rules 2022 — Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change

=== WHAT IS E-WASTE? ===
Electronic waste includes all electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) that has been discarded:
Category 1 — IT and Telecom: Laptops, desktops, tablets, mobile phones, smart watches, printers, scanners, fax machines, keyboards, mouse, hard drives, routers, modems, cables, chargers, power banks
Category 2 — Consumer Electronics: TVs (CRT, LCD, LED, OLED), cameras, DVD players, speakers, projectors, gaming consoles, remote controls
Category 3 — Large Appliances: Refrigerators, washing machines, dishwashers, air conditioners, microwave ovens, water purifiers (RO systems)
Category 4 — Small Appliances: Mixers, grinders, iron, fans, hair dryers, electric kettles, toasters, geysers
Category 5 — Lighting: CFL bulbs, fluorescent tubes, LED lights, sodium vapour lamps
Category 6 — Medical: X-ray machines, blood analysers, ECG machines (dispose via hospital waste channels)
Category 7 — Solar Panels: Old solar panels, inverters, charge controllers (EPR responsibility of manufacturer)

=== WHY E-WASTE IS DANGEROUS — DO NOT DUMP IN REGULAR BIN ===
E-waste contains toxic substances:
- Lead (solder, CRT screens): damages brain and nervous system, especially children
- Mercury (CFL/fluorescent bulbs, switches): causes kidney failure, neurological damage
- Cadmium (batteries, older LCD screens): carcinogenic, accumulates in kidneys
- Chromium (metal coatings): causes lung cancer
- Brominated flame retardants (circuit boards): endocrine disruptors
- Arsenic (LED chips): toxic to multiple organs
- PVC insulation: releases dioxins when burned

NEVER:
- Throw e-waste in green or blue bin — contaminates organic/recyclable waste
- Burn e-waste — toxic fumes cause respiratory disease and cancer
- Give to unorganised scrap dealers who manually break apart — child labour + toxic exposure
- Dump in open — leaches toxins into groundwater

=== HOW TO DISPOSE E-WASTE IN INDIA ===
OPTION 1 — MANUFACTURER TAKE-BACK (Best Option)
Under E-Waste Rules 2022, all manufacturers have EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) obligation:
- Apple: apple.com/in/shop/trade-in or nearest Apple Authorised Service Provider
- Samsung: samsung.com/in/sustainability/e-waste (or visit Samsung Experience Store)
- Dell: dell.com/en-in/dt/corporate/social-responsibility/e-waste-recycling.htm
- HP: hp.com/in — HP Planet Partners programme
- LG: Authorised LG service centres accept old LG products
- Sony: Authorised Sony centres — check website for collection drives
Most manufacturers offer: Free pickup, discount on new purchase, or gift vouchers

=== SPECIFIC ITEM DISPOSAL ===

MOBILE PHONES AND SMARTPHONES:
Before disposal:
1. Factory reset the phone (Settings > System > Reset)
2. Remove SIM card and memory card
3. Remove case/cover
4. Charge battery to 30% (for safe handling)
Options: Sell on Cashify/OLX (if working), drop at BBMP DWCC, book Attero pickup, or give to manufacturer store

LAPTOP AND COMPUTERS:
Before disposal:
1. Back up all data to external drive or cloud
2. Wipe hard drive (use software like DBAN for HDD; for SSD use manufacturer secure erase)
3. Remove battery if removable
Options: Cashify/OLX, Dell/HP/Lenovo take-back, Attero pickup, Cerebra recyclers

CFL AND FLUORESCENT BULBS (CONTAIN MERCURY):
NEVER break, crush, or put in regular bin.
If a CFL breaks: Ventilate the room, pick up large pieces with gloves, use damp paper towel for dust, seal in zip-lock bag, dispose at DWCC
Disposal: BBMP DWCC, electronics shops (many take back old bulbs), Philips/Syska authorised dealers
Safe alternative: Switch to LED bulbs — no mercury, 80% less energy

BATTERIES:
DO NOT put in dry blue bin (contain heavy metals).
Types:
- AA/AAA/C/D cells (alkaline): Red bin or DWCC
- Lithium-ion (phone, laptop): Manufacturer take-back ONLY — fire hazard
- Lead-acid car batteries: Mechanic/battery dealer — mandatory take-back in India
- Button cells (watch, hearing aid): Jeweller or DWCC
- Rechargeable NiMH/NiCd: DWCC or e-waste recycler

AIR CONDITIONERS (AC):
Contain refrigerant gases (R-22, R-410A) — must be handled by certified technician.
NEVER dump or dismantle yourself — refrigerant release causes ozone depletion.
Disposal: Call AC brand service centre for decommissioning, or sell to authorised scrap dealer with CPCB certificate.

=== E-WASTE RULES 2022 — KEY PROVISIONS ===
1. All producers must meet annual collection targets (starts 60% in 2023-24, increases 10%/year)
2. Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) certificate mandatory
3. Producers must publish list of authorised recyclers on their website
4. Refurbishers must register with State Pollution Control Board
5. Penalty for non-compliance: Suspension of EPR certificate + fine up to Rs. 1,00,000
6. Informal sector workers must register with State PCB to legally handle e-waste

=== E-WASTE STATISTICS — INDIA ===
- India generates ~3.2 million tonnes of e-waste per year (3rd largest globally after China and USA)
- Only ~22% is formally recycled (target: 60% by 2024)
- 95% currently handled by informal sector (unregulated)
- Bangalore alone generates ~92,000 tonnes/year
- Major informal e-waste hubs: Shivaji Nagar (Bangalore), Seelampur (Delhi), Dharavi (Mumbai)
