ORGANIC WASTE AND COMPOSTING GUIDE FOR INDIA
BBMP Sahaaya Programme + SWM Rules 2016 Compliance

=== WHAT IS WET/ORGANIC WASTE? ===
Biodegradable waste that can be composted or converted to biogas:

YES — Put in GREEN BIN:
- Vegetable peels and scraps (potato, onion, carrot, etc.)
- Fruit peels and cores (banana, mango, apple, citrus — all okay)
- Cooked food leftovers (rice, dal, roti, curry, bread)
- Uncooked food scraps (wilted vegetables, spoiled fruit)
- Eggshells (excellent compost additive — calcium rich)
- Tea bags and tea leaves, coffee grounds and filter paper
- Fish bones and small animal bones
- Garden waste: dry leaves, grass clippings, flower waste, small twigs
- Soiled paper napkins, paper bags with food stains
- Coconut shells (dried), sugarcane pulp

NO — Do NOT put in green bin:
- Plastic bags or wrappers (even "biodegradable" — slow to decompose)
- Glass, metal, rubber
- Sanitary napkins, diapers (RED bin)
- CFL bulbs, batteries (RED bin)
- Large quantities of oil or liquid (absorb in newspaper first)
- Dead animals (requires special disposal — contact BBMP)

=== COMPOSTING AT HOME — STEP BY STEP ===
Home composting is the best way to reduce wet waste going to landfills.

METHOD 1: POT/TERRACOTTA COMPOSTING (Apartments — most practical)
What you need: 2-3 clay or plastic pots (10–15 litre), cocopeat or dry leaves, kitchen waste
Steps:
1. Layer 2 inches of cocopeat/dry leaves at bottom
2. Add kitchen waste (vegetable peels, fruit scraps, cooked food) — 1-2 cups at a time
3. Sprinkle thin layer of cocopeat/dry soil on top
4. Repeat layers; keep lid loosely placed (not airtight — needs air)
5. Rotate between pots: when Pot 1 is full, switch to Pot 2 while Pot 1 matures
6. Compost is ready in 45–60 days — dark brown, earthy smell, crumbly texture
7. Use in pots, garden beds, donated to BBMP's urban farming

TIPS:
- Chop/shred waste into smaller pieces — speeds decomposition by 50%
- Maintain Carbon:Nitrogen ratio — for every bucket of wet waste (N), add half bucket dry leaves/cocopeat (C)
- Too wet = add dry leaves; too dry = sprinkle water
- Mild earthy smell is normal; foul/rotten smell means too wet or anaerobic — add dry leaves and mix

METHOD 2: KHAMBA COMPOSTING (Traditional Indian Method)
Khamba = 3-stacked terracotta pots with holes, traditional Indian composting vessel
Used widely in Mumbai (Kavitha Kuruganti's initiative) and spreading to Bangalore
Works same as pot composting but more efficient due to aeration holes
BBMP distributes free Khambas under Sahaaya programme — contact your ward office

METHOD 3: VERMICOMPOSTING (Worm Composting)
Uses earthworms (Eisenia fetida — red wigglers) to accelerate decomposition
Produces "vermicast" — highest quality organic fertiliser (better than chemical NPK)
Steps:
1. Get a wooden or plastic bin with drainage holes at bottom
2. Add bedding: shredded newspaper + cocopeat (moistened)
3. Add 100–200 earthworms (available from gardening stores, Rs. 200–400 per kg)
4. Add kitchen waste in one corner; worms will migrate there
5. Cover waste with damp newspaper or cardboard
6. Harvest vermicast after 60–90 days from the oldest section
Ideal for: Apartments with balcony, homes with garden
Sells for: Rs. 15–40 per kg (if you have surplus)
Bangalore suppliers: Hasiru Dala, Organic Mandya, Cubbon Park Nursery

METHOD 4: BIOGAS PLANT (Societies and Bulk Generators)
Biogas plants convert wet waste into cooking gas (methane) and slurry (fertiliser)
Recommended for: Apartments >50 units, schools, hospitals, restaurants
Capacity: 1 kg wet waste → 100 litres of biogas (enough for 30 min cooking)
Cost: Rs. 30,000–1,50,000 depending on size (subsidy available under MNRE scheme)
Installation: BBMP empanelled vendors; free technical support from Karnataka Renewable Energy Development Ltd (KREDL)
Biogas slurry: Excellent liquid fertiliser — dilute 1:10 with water for plants

Collection → Pourakarmika collects segregated wet waste in green compartment of 3-compartment vehicle
↓
Transfer Station → Weighed and logged at Mini Transfer Station (MTS)
↓
Processing Facility → One of 11 BBMP biomethanation plants or 3,200+ small composting units

- Doddabidarakallu: 350 TPD capacity, produces electricity + compost
- Lingadheeranahalli: 350 TPD, biomethanation
- Subramanyapura: Windrow composting, 100 TPD

Output → Compost sold to farmers at Rs. 4–6/kg | Biogas used in plant operations | Slurry used in BBMP gardens

=== ORGANIC WASTE — DO's AND DON'Ts ===
DO:
✓ Segregate wet waste from dry — keep green bin separate
✓ Drain excess liquid before putting in bin (prevents leakage, controls odour)
✓ Wrap cooked non-vegetarian waste in newspaper — reduces smell
✓ Start composting — even a small 5-litre pot makes a difference
✓ Use compost in balcony plants — reduces need for chemical fertiliser
✓ Shred large pieces before composting — faster breakdown

DON'T:
✗ Mix plastic bags with food waste — ruins the entire batch
✗ Put large quantities of oil/liquid directly in green bin
✗ Throw wet waste in dry bin or vice versa — defeats segregation
✗ Leave uncovered wet bin outside for long — attracts flies and pests
✗ Burn dry leaves — compost them instead

=== ORGANIC WASTE — STATISTICS ===
- Wet/organic waste = ~55% of total municipal solid waste in India
- Bangalore generates ~5,000 tonnes of wet waste per day
- Currently processed: ~40% (rest goes to open dumping grounds)
- Home composting potential: If all Bangalore households composted, ~600 TPD diverted from landfills
- Methane emissions from landfilling food waste: 2.5–3.5x more potent than CO2
- Composting 1 kg food waste prevents ~0.6 kg CO2-equivalent emissions
